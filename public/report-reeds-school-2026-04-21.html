Reed's School

Report date: 21 April 2026  ·  Cobham, Surrey, United Kingdom
Reed's is a settled, financially solid co-educational school in prosperous Surrey commuter country, built around a 200-year-old charitable Foundation that still educates roughly 85 pupils who have lost the support of a parent. It does boys' secondary education from 11 to 16 and then opens up to girls in the sixth form, and the rhythm of the school is firmly day-school — around 90% of pupils go home each evening. If you are looking for a heavily international, full-boarding, Oxbridge-factory experience, this is not that school; if you want a well-resourced English independent with strong sport, solid A-Levels, and a recently praised pastoral culture, it is squarely on the list.

Curriculum & Academic Outcomes

The academic offer is traditional and broad. In the first two years your child would take a wide common curriculum taught by specialist teachers. GCSE choice follows — pupils sit either nine or ten GCSEs/IGCSEs with a required core of English, Maths, sciences and a modern language, plus three freely chosen options. Sixth Form is A-Level (three subjects, with a fourth in Further Maths or a native language available) alongside an elective programme that can add an EPQ, an AS-Level, or a BTEC.

The headline figure is 51% of A-Level grades at A or A* in 2025, with 85% at A*–B (source: school's own results page). That is a strong outcome, though worth reading in context: around half of A-Levels at A/A* is the kind of result you see at good, academically selective, non-hothouse independents. It is not the 70%+ A/A* ratio you see at the most academically pressured schools — which for many families is a feature, not a bug. GCSEs in 2025 saw 75% of grades at 7–9 and 25% at grade 9.

On university destinations, the 2025 leavers produced 4 Cambridge acceptances and, strikingly, no Oxford acceptances listed, against 130 Russell Group places (source). The strongest pipelines run to Bath, Birmingham, Newcastle, Nottingham, Exeter, Manchester and Durham. Eight pupils took up medicine, dentistry or veterinary offers.

Admissions

Boys apply at 11+ (Year 7) or 13+ (Year 9). Girls apply at 16+ only. The entrance exam is the ISEB Common Pre-Test (a standardised online test in English, Maths and reasoning used by many UK private schools for 11+/13+ entry) taken in Year 6, followed by an interview and a group activity day. At Sixth Form, candidates sit two subject papers based on their chosen A-Levels, plus an interview.

Key dates for the next live cycle (2027 entry): registration closes 15 October 2026 for 11+ and 13+; 2 October 2026 for Sixth Form. The non-refundable registration fee is £180. Security deposit on acceptance is £1,500 (11+) or £3,000 (13+). There is no GCSE grade floor published publicly, but the guidance is "a wide range of grades mainly at 6, 7, 8 and 9" for Sixth Form.

Student Community Profile

Roughly 850 pupils, split 90% day and 10% boarding. The school has not published its international pupil percentage, nationality mix, or English-as-an-Additional-Language figures on any page we could find; the ISI report records 19 pupils with EAL (about 2%) and 295 pupils identified as having SEND (Special Educational Needs and Disabilities — pupils needing extra learning support), which is around 35% of the school. That SEND figure is high and worth contextualising on a tour: it reflects broad inclusion including mild dyslexia, dyspraxia and ADHD, not heavy specialist provision — only 2 pupils hold an EHC plan (Education, Health and Care plan — a legal document for children with significant additional needs).

If you are an international family, the school does not publicly state how many pupils are non-British. Ask directly.

Pastoral Care & Facilities

The ISI inspection highlighted pastoral care as a genuine strength. The school runs a "House" system, vertical tutor groups in Middle School, and three separate boarding houses — The Close for Years 7–8, School House for Years 9–11, and a refurbished co-ed Sixth Form House with single occupancy and separate girls' and boys' wings.

Facilities are the area where Reed's is unusually well-resourced for a school of 850. Sport alone has: a five-lane indoor cricket centre with PitchVision analysis, two floodlit astroturf pitches, indoor and outdoor tennis courts, a 25-metre pool, a multi-fitness gym, squash courts, and rugby and cricket pitches across 40 acres. The FutureTech building is the school's flagship STEM space. Academic Support is in a central, purpose-designed suite — not bolted on.

Location & Getting Here

Reed's sits in Cobham, in prosperous Surrey commuter country — green, quiet, closer to Oxshott village than Cobham itself. The feel is leafy and affluent rather than rural. The site is 40 acres of heath and woodland.

Nearest station: Oxshott, 0.9 km away — about a 10-minute walk through Oxshott Heath (the school notes this can be muddy in wet weather). The station is on the Waterloo–Guildford line via Cobham.

Financial Health

What this charity is. The charity Reed's School operates under is legally named "THE LONDON ORPHAN ASYLUM (REED'S SCHOOL)" — the archaic "London Orphan Asylum" wording is the original 1813 charitable name, and the working name is simply Reed's School. This IS the operating entity for the school you are considering; it is not a separate foundation or an umbrella covering multiple schools. The numbers below describe the school itself.

Before the table, three definitions:

  • Total assets = everything the school owns (buildings, playing fields, cash in the bank, investments).
  • Total liabilities = everything it owes (loans, pension commitments, future costs it has signed up to).
  • Net position = assets minus liabilities. Higher is healthier.

Three things jump out. First, the school ran a small operating surplus every year in the period — income exceeded expenditure by around £0.8m–£5m each year. Second, income grew steadily from £20.8m in 2021 to £27.0m in 2024 — a healthy trajectory. Third, and this is the striking one: between August 2023 and August 2024, total liabilities more than doubled, from £12.35m to £30.23m, while assets jumped from £46.3m to £65.6m.

In plain language: a liability doubling in a single year sounds alarming but is not automatically bad. It typically means the school took out a loan to build something, or a long-term property lease was brought onto the balance sheet. The key fact: assets grew faster than liabilities — the net position actually improved from £33.96m to £35.39m. The school is not quietly eroding its reserves.

What parents should ask: what was the 2024 balance-sheet event? A capital project, a long-term lease now shown as debt, or borrowing for a specific build? The numbers are consistent with healthy investment, but this is the single specific money question worth raising on a tour.

Inspection Record

Reed's was inspected by the ISI on 21–23 January 2025 — now 15 months old, well within the typical 3–5 year cycle. An eight-person team spent two and a half days on site.

The verdict was unambiguous:

"Standards relating to leadership and management, and governance are met. Standards relating to the quality of education, training and recreation are met. Standards relating to pupils' physical and mental health and emotional wellbeing are met. Standards relating to pupils' social and economic education and contribution to society are met. Standards relating to safeguarding are met."

The report also awarded a "significant strength" — a plaudit ISI reserves for aspects they consider unusual on a national basis:

"Teachers deliver engaging lessons and provide consistent high-quality feedback to pupils. Pupils are encouraged to self-reflect and develop targets for improvement. This highly effective personalised feedback has led to sustained improvement in examination results and outcomes for all pupils. This is a significant strength of the school."

Pastoral quality was also praised:

"Effective leadership ensures that the school provides a nurturing, inclusive environment where pupils thrive academically, socially and personally."

There was one recommendation for improvement:

"Leaders should develop the consistency of the school's processes for communication between subject teachers and the SEND department so the provision to meet the needs of pupils who have SEND is effective."

The inspectors also flagged mildly that "in some lessons in mathematics and science, off-task behaviour is not consistently challenged by staff" — the only negative note in the entire report.

For a parent whose child has a learning difference, that SEND-communication recommendation is the single data point to probe: the school has 295 SEND pupils on its register, and the inspectors said integration between specialist staff and classroom teachers is not always up-to-date. Ask the Academic Support department directly how they have responded in the 15 months since.

Accreditation & Association Status

Reed's is a member of the Headmasters' and Headmistresses' Conference (HMC) and inspected by ISI. The school does not appear to hold COBIS, CIS or IBO accreditation, which is consistent with its positioning as a UK-curriculum school rather than an international school. It trades on HMC membership and the ISI "significant strength," not on a basket of international badges.

Wellbeing & Mental Health Staffing

The published wellbeing team comprises 2 counsellors, 3 school nurses, and 1 chaplain — 6 people in total for a pupil population of around 850. That gives a ratio of roughly 1 wellbeing staff member per 142 pupils.

For context, the UK NHS CAMHS (Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services — NHS mental health care for young people) target is a 4-week wait from referral. Independent boarding schools typically aim for 1 counsellor per 150–300 pupils. At 2 counsellors for 850 pupils that is 1 counsellor per 425 pupils — worse than the typical independent-school benchmark, though the total team including nurses and chaplain brings it within the wider range.

What the school does not publish: the typical wait time between a pupil requesting a counselling session and actually seeing someone, or whether the counsellors are on site every day. Worth asking on a tour.

School Policies

The school publishes an Anti-Bullying Policy, Social Media Policy, Online Safety Policy, Countering Cyberbullying Policy, and Behaviour Rewards and Sanctions Policy on its Policies page. The bullying policy is online and downloadable — not on-request only — a positive signal of transparency.

What we could not find: a specific phone-and-device policy for day pupils or boarding houses. Most top UK boarding schools operate phone-free dorms after lights-out. Ask where Reed's sits on this, particularly for the Years 7–8 Close boarding house where pupils are still 11 and 12.

Safeguarding & Governance Signals

There are no Charity Commission serious incident reports on file for this school.

Teacher Regulation Agency (TRA) check. The TRA is the government body that bans teachers found guilty of misconduct. We ran the TRA publications search for "Reed's School" and verified every hit against the school profile. Of 15 raw search hits, zero were confirmed to relate to staff at this school; four were dropped as clear keyword false-positives. Parents who want to run the check themselves can do so at the TRA publications feed.

Trustee count — the "only 2 trustees" question. The Charity Commission register lists just two named trustees: Dr John Chinegwundoh and Dr Alison McLean. On the face of it, 2 trustees for a £27m-a-year school sounds low. Many historic UK schools file only a narrow subset of their governing body as "trustees" with the Charity Commission, while the full governing Council runs to 12–20 members. Reed's publishes a full governors list including around 17 names. Ask the Chair how the legal trustee count maps to the full Council.

Local Safety Context

The area around Reed's is safe by UK and international standards. Before any parent outside the UK reads the raw numbers, some important framing.

UK "violent crime" is a wide category. In UK police data, "violent crime" includes any unwanted physical contact — a push, a verbal threat, a shoplifter brushing a guard. Roughly 70–80% of UK violent-crime incidents involve no injury or only minor injury. This is not comparable to "violent crime" in Bangkok or Singapore, where the term typically means serious assault only.

In the 12 months to January 2026, within a 1-mile radius of the school, police recorded:

Anti-social behaviour means noise complaints, public rowdiness, groups loitering — not physical threat. Public order means shouting, offensive language in public, or being drunk and disorderly.

A parent from Bangkok reading "40% violent crime" would naturally be alarmed. They shouldn't be. The UK definition captures incidents that Thai police would not record as crimes at all. Put in per-1,000-residents terms, this area sits at roughly 55–65 incidents per 1,000 residents per year — below the England & Wales average of about 85, and at the lower end of Surrey's typical range.

By international comparison:

  • Singapore: ~6–8 per 1,000
  • Hong Kong: ~10–15 per 1,000
  • Bangkok (official): ~20–25 per 1,000 (widely thought to be 2–3× higher in reality due to underreporting, and with a narrower definition)
  • USA average: ~40–50 per 1,000
  • England & Wales: ~85 per 1,000
  • This area: ~55–65 per 1,000

Burglary at 9% is unremarkable — there is no elevated category here that would cause a practical concern.

One specific tour question: what are the school's security arrangements at the gate and around the boarding houses, especially at night and during weekend exeats?

5 Questions to Ask on a Tour

  1. The 2024 balance-sheet event "Your 2024 accounts show total liabilities rising from £12.3m to £30.2m in one year, while assets jumped from £46.3m to £65.6m. What was the underlying event — a capital project, a long-term lease brought onto the balance sheet, or borrowing for a specific build?"
  2. The registered trustee count "The Charity Commission register lists only two named trustees, while your website lists a full Council of around 17. How does the legal trustee count map to the Council, and who holds primary charity-trustee responsibility?"
  3. SEND integration (the one ISI recommendation) "The January 2025 ISI report recommended improving communication between subject teachers and the SEND department. What has specifically changed in the 15 months since, and how is that measured?"
  4. Wellbeing waiting time "With 2 counsellors for around 850 pupils, what is the typical wait between a pupil asking for a counselling session and actually seeing someone?"
  5. Oxbridge and destinations "Your 2025 destinations show 4 Cambridge acceptances and no Oxford. Is Oxford simply not applied to in large numbers, or is this a year-on-year dip?"
